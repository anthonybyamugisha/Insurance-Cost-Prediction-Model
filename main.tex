\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

Accurate prediction of medical insurance costs remains a long standing challenge in actuarial science, with important implications for both insurers and policyholders. Traditional statistical methods, while interpretable, often struggle to capture complex non linear relationships between demographic and health related variables. This report presents the end to end development of a machine learning system designed to address this limitation by predicting individual annual insurance charges from a set of policyholder attributes.

Six modelling approaches were developed and evaluated on a dataset obtained from Hugging Face (\texttt{adegoke655/Insurance}), consisting of 1,338 records. After removing one duplicate entry, 1,337 unique observations were retained for analysis. The models included Linear Regression, Support Vector Regression (SVR), Random Forest, Gradient Boosting, XGBoost, and a Deep Neural Network (DNN). All models were trained under the same conditions, using an 80/20 train-test split, 5 fold cross-validation, and grid-search hyperparameter tuning to ensure a fair comparison.

XGBoost achieved the best performance with a test $R^2$ score of 0.902, using 15 shallow trees with a maximum depth of 3 and requiring less than one second of training time. The Deep Neural Network achieved a test $R^2$ score of 0.816, indicating that tree-based ensemble methods performed better on this small tabular dataset. SHAP analysis showed that smoking status was the most influential feature (57.7\%), followed by age (22.5\%), BMI (14.5\%), and number of children (5.3\%). Gender and region were removed during feature selection due to minimal predictive value, and SHAP analysis indicated no strong dependence on these variables in the final model.

\section{Overview}
Health insurance premium calculation presents a compelling challenge at the intersection of data science and actuarial practice. This report documents the end to end development of a machine learning system capable of predicting individual medical insurance costs from policyholder characteristics. The exponential growth of healthcare data and advances in computational methods have opened new avenues for building more accurate, fair, and transparent pricing systems that go beyond traditional actuarial tables. By applying modern machine learning techniques to a structured dataset of policyholder attributes, this project demonstrates how data-driven models can deliver both predictive power and interpretability in a domain where trust and explainability are paramount. The resulting system is not only validated against rigorous statistical benchmarks but is also deployed as an interactive web application, bridging the gap between machine learning research and practical real-world utility.

\section{Background of the Problem}
Health insurance premium calculation is a complex regression problem involving multiple demographic and health related factors. Traditional actuarial methods rely on statistical models that may not capture non-linear interactions between features. The rising cost of healthcare and the need for transparent, personalised pricing make accurate premium prediction a high-value problem for both insurers and policyholders.

This project leverages machine learning to provide accurate, interpretable predictions of insurance costs from individual policyholder characteristics.

\section{Problem Statement}
Given a set of policyholder attributes that is age, sex, BMI, number of children, smoking status, and residential region, the task is to build a regression model that accurately predicts annual insurance charges. The model must be both accurate ($R^2 \geq 0.90$) and interpretable, so that predictions can be explained to end users.

\chapter{Literature Review}

\section{Machine Learning in Insurance Pricing}
Traditional insurance pricing relies on generalised linear models (GLMs), particularly Tweedie regression, which assumes a specific distributional form for claims data \cite{pedregosa2011}. While GLMs offer statistical interpretability, they struggle to capture non            c  linear interactions among policyholder characteristics for example, the multiplicative effect of smoking and BMI on healthcare utilisation.

There is growing evidence that machine learning models improve on GLMs in claims prediction tasks. Gradient boosted trees, in particular, have emerged as a strong approach for structured tabular insurance data due to their ability to model complex interactions without manual feature engineering \cite{chen2016xgboost}. Random Forests have also been widely applied, though they tend to overfit on smaller datasets and produce less sharp feature attribution compared to boosting methods.

\section{Ensemble Methods and Gradient Boosting}
Gradient Boosting Machines (GBMs), introduced by Friedman \cite{friedman2001greedy}, build an additive model of weak learners sequentially, with each tree trained to correct the residual errors of the previous ensemble. XGBoost \cite{chen2016xgboost} extended this framework with second-order gradient optimisation, column subsampling, and built in $L_1/L_2$ regularisation, making it both more accurate and more resistant to overfitting than earlier GBM implementations. On small datasets ($n < 10{,}000$), shallow XGBoost configurations with few trees and low depth are particularly competitive against deep learning architectures, which typically require large volumes of data to generalise effectively \cite{grinsztajn2022tree}.

\section{Deep Learning for Tabular Data}
Despite advances in deep learning for images, text, and time series, neural networks have not consistently outperformed tree  based ensembles on tabular regression tasks. Grinsztajn et al.\ \cite{grinsztajn2022tree} conducted a systematic benchmark and found that tree-based models outperform MLPs on the majority of tabular datasets, particularly those with fewer than 50,000 samples. A key reason is that DNNs require careful feature scaling, large sample sizes, and extensive hyperparameter tuning to generalise well conditions that are rarely met on small structured datasets. Kernel-based methods such as SVR face similar sensitivity to unscaled inputs, which directly anticipates the failure of the RBF kernel SVR observed in this project. This evidence motivates the selection of XGBoost as the primary model.

\section{Model Interpretability and SHAP}
Interpretability is critical in insurance applications, where regulators and policyholders may demand explanations for pricing decisions. SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations), introduced by Lundberg and Lee \cite{lundberg2017unified}, provides a theoretically grounded framework for attributing a model's prediction to individual input features using concepts from cooperative game theory. Unlike permutation importance or gain-based feature importance, SHAP values are consistent and locally accurate, meaning they correctly reflect each feature's marginal contribution to a specific prediction \cite{lundberg2017unified}. SHAP has been adopted broadly in actuarial and financial modelling as the standard tool for regulatory explainability.

\section{Fairness in Predictive Insurance Models}
The interpretability tools discussed above are closely linked to fairness: a model whose predictions can be attributed feature by feature also allows auditors to verify that no demographic variable is driving outcomes inappropriately. The use of gender or ethnicity as direct or proxy predictors raises legal and ethical concerns in many jurisdictions \cite{cohen1988statistical}. Machine learning models can inadvertently encode such biases if uninformative but correlated features are retained. Feature selection grounded in predictive gain, combined with SHAP based post hoc validation \cite{lundberg2017unified}, provides a practical mechanism for detecting and eliminating demographic bias from model predictions.

\section{Knowledge Gap}
The sections above establish that tree based ensembles and SHAP driven interpretability are well supported in the literature, yet two practical gaps remain relevant to this project. First, the majority of published studies are conducted on large proprietary datasets held by established insurers, with little work evaluating model performance in the small data regime (fewer than 2,000 records) typical of academic benchmarks \cite{grinsztajn2022tree}. The behaviour of XGBoost under these constraints particularly the tradeoff between tree depth and generalisation is not well characterised in the insurance domain.

Second, few studies integrate the complete modelling pipeline  feature selection, hyperparameter optimisation, SHAP based interpretability, fairness validation, and live deployment within a single reproducible project. Most work addresses only one or two of these stages, leaving a gap in end to end pipeline design for small scale insurance applications. Both gaps directly motivate the scope and methodological choices described in the following chapter.

\subsection{Duplicate Removal}
The raw dataset contained 1,338 records. One exact duplicate row was identified and removed, yielding \textbf{1,337 unique records} for all subsequent steps.

\subsection{Missing Value Check}
A systematic check across all seven columns confirmed \textbf{no missing values} in the dataset. No imputation was therefore required.

\subsection{Outlier Treatment (BMI)}
BMI outliers were detected using the Interquartile Range (IQR) method and treated by winsorisation (capping):

\begin{equation}
  IQR = Q_3 - Q_1 = 34.69 - 26.30 = 8.39, \quad
  \text{Bounds: } [13.67,\ 47.31]
\end{equation}

Values outside this range were capped using \texttt{ArbitraryOutlierCapper} from the \texttt{feature engine} library. Winsorisation was preferred over deletion to preserve sample size.

  \item \textbf{Primary metric:} $R^2$ (coefficient of determination)

\begin{equation}
  R^2 = 1 - \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i - \bar{y})^2}
\end{equation}

\chapter{Model Development}

\section{Linear Regression}

Linear Regression serves as the baseline model for this project. It assumes a linear relationship between the input features and insurance charges, estimating a weighted sum of the predictors \cite{pedregosa2011}. Despite its simplicity, it provides a useful performance benchmark against which more complex models are compared.

\begin{equation}
  \hat{y} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{\text{age}} + \beta_2 x_{\text{bmi}}
           + \beta_3 x_{\text{children}} + \beta_4 x_{\text{smoker}}
\end{equation}

Estimated coefficients: $\hat{\beta}_{\text{smoker}} \approx 12{,}658$,
$\hat{\beta}_{\text{age}} \approx 388$, $\hat{\beta}_{\text{bmi}} \approx 676$,
$\hat{\beta}_{\text{children}} \approx 425$.

\textbf{Result:} Train $R^2 = 0.730$, Test $R^2 = 0.806$. Underfitting due to
unmodelled non-linear interactions between smoking, BMI, and age.

\section{Support Vector Regressor (SVR)}

Support Vector Regression extends the principles of Support Vector Machines to regression tasks by finding a hyperplane that fits the data within a specified margin of tolerance ($\varepsilon$) \cite{pedregosa2011}. An RBF kernel was selected to capture potential non-linear relationships between features and charges, though this choice is highly sensitive to input feature scale.

\begin{equation}
  \min_{\mathbf{w},b,\xi} \frac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{w}\|^2 + C\sum_{i=1}^{n}(\xi_i + \xi_i^*)
\end{equation}

\textbf{Result:} Train $R^2 = -0.102$, Test $R^2 = -0.134$.
\textcolor{failred}{\textbf{Failed}} entirely due to unscaled input features;
the RBF kernel is highly sensitive to feature scale.

\section{Random Forest}

Random Forest is an ensemble method that constructs multiple decision trees on bootstrapped subsets of the training data and averages their predictions \cite{pedregosa2011}. By introducing randomness in both data sampling and feature selection at each split, it reduces variance and generally outperforms single decision trees on tabular datasets.

\begin{equation}
  \hat{y}_{\text{RF}}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^{B} f_b(\mathbf{x})
\end{equation}

Grid search selected 120 trees (\texttt{n\_estimators=120}). \textbf{Result:}
Test $R^2 = 0.882$, but significant overfitting (train $R^2 = 0.975$, gap $= -0.093$).

\section{Gradient Boosting}

Gradient Boosting builds an ensemble of weak learners sequentially, where each new tree is trained to correct the residual errors of the previous ensemble \cite{friedman2001greedy}. The learning rate $\eta$ controls the contribution of each tree, balancing model complexity against the risk of overfitting on the training data.

\begin{equation}
  F_m(\mathbf{x}) = F_{m-1}(\mathbf{x}) + \eta \cdot h_m(\mathbf{x})
\end{equation}

Grid search selected \texttt{learning\_rate}$=0.2$, \texttt{n\_estimators}$=21$.
\textbf{Result:} Test $R^2 = 0.902$; well calibrated with a small overfitting gap
of $+0.034$.

\section{XGBoost}

XGBoost (Extreme Gradient Boosting) extends standard gradient boosting with second-order gradient optimisation, built-in $L_1/L_2$ regularisation via the $\Omega$ term, and column subsampling \cite{chen2016xgboost}. These additions make it significantly more resistant to overfitting and computationally faster than traditional GBM implementations, particularly on small structured datasets.

\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}(\theta) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \ell(y_i, \hat{y}_i)
  + \sum_{k=1}^{K} \Omega(f_k), \quad
  \Omega(f_k) = \gamma T + \frac{1}{2}\lambda\sum_{j=1}^{T} w_j^2
\end{equation}

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{XGBoost Hyperparameter Grid Search}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
\textbf{Parameter} & \textbf{Values Tested} & \textbf{Optimal} \\
\midrule
\texttt{n\_estimators} & 10, 15, 20, 40, 50    & \textbf{15} \\
\texttt{max\_depth}    & 3, 4, 5               & \textbf{3}  \\
\texttt{gamma}         & 0, 0.15, 0.3, 0.5, 1  & \textbf{0}  \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The optimal leaf weight and split gain at each iteration are:

\begin{equation}
  w_j^* = -\frac{\sum_{i \in I_j} g_i}{\sum_{i \in I_j} h_i + \lambda}, \qquad
  \text{Gain} = \frac{1}{2}\left[\frac{G_L^2}{H_L+\lambda}
  + \frac{G_R^2}{H_R+\lambda} - \frac{G^2}{H+\lambda}\right] - \gamma
\end{equation}

\textbf{Result:} Test $R^2 = 0.902$, CV $R^2 = 0.861$, gap $= +0.033$.
Selected as \textbf{champion model}.

\section{SHAP Interpretability Analysis}

\subsection{SHAP Theory}
SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) attributes each prediction to individual features
using cooperative game theory:

\begin{equation}
  \phi_j(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{S \subseteq F \setminus \{j\}}
  \frac{|S|!\,(|F|-|S|-1)!}{|F|!}
  \left[ f_{S \cup \{j\}}(\mathbf{x}_{S \cup \{j\}}) - f_S(\mathbf{x}_S) \right],
  \quad
  f(\mathbf{x}) = \phi_0 + \sum_{j=1}^{|F|} \phi_j(\mathbf{x})
\end{equation}

\subsection{Key SHAP Insights}
\begin{enumerate}

  \item \textbf{Age (22.5\%):} Nearly $6\times$ higher than the gain estimate of 3.9\%,

  \item \textbf{BMI (14.5\%):} Mainly differentiates cost tiers among smokers.
  \item \textbf{Children (5.3\%):} Minimal but non-zero marginal effect.
\end{enumerate}

\chapter{Conclusion and Future Work}

\section{Summary of Findings}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{XGBoost achieved test $R^2 = 0.902$}, meeting the $\geq 90\%$ objective
        with only 15 trees of depth 3.
  \item \textbf{Smoking is the dominant predictor} (57.7\% SHAP). Smokers pay
        $\approx 3.8\times$ more than non-smokers.
  \item \textbf{Age (22.5\%) and BMI (14.5\%)} are secondary predictors; SHAP reveals
        age's true importance far exceeds XGBoost's gain estimate.
  \item \textbf{The model is fair}: gender and region were removed automatically;
        SHAP confirms no demographic bias.
  \item \textbf{Gender analysis}: males pay an average of 11\% more than females,
        but Cohen's $d \approx 0.13$ confirms this is a negligible practical effect.
  \item \textbf{Deep learning does not outperform XGBoost} on this small tabular
        dataset (DNN: $R^2 = 0.816$), consistent with the literature.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Limitations}

\section{Future Work}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{F\arabic*.}]

  \item \textbf{Extending the Model to the Ugandan Context:} The current model is
        trained on a United States based dataset with charges denominated in USD,
        which limits its direct applicability to the Ugandan insurance market.
        Future work should incorporate locally sourced data from Ugandan insurers
        (e.g.\ UAP, Jubilee, Britam Uganda) with premiums expressed in Uganda
        Shillings (UGX), and account for region specific factors such as proximity
        to public health facilities, malaria and HIV prevalence rates, the
        urban rural healthcare cost divide, and out of pocket expenditure patterns.
        Partnering with the Insurance Regulatory Authority of Uganda (IRA) to access
        anonymised claims data would be a practical and impactful first step toward
        a locally calibrated pricing model.

  \item \textbf{Prediction Intervals:} Replacing point predictions with calibrated
        confidence intervals via conformal prediction or quantile XGBoost
        would give policyholders a range of expected costs rather than a single
        figure, improving transparency and trust.

  \item \textbf{MLOps Pipeline:} Implementing model versioning (MLflow), automated
        retraining triggers, and drift monitoring (Evidently AI) would bring the
        system to production grade and ensure predictions remain reliable as
        policyholder demographics shift over time.

  \item \textbf{API Backend:} Replacing the Streamlit only deployment with a FastAPI
        REST backend would enable integration with insurer CRMs, mobile applications,
        and third party quoting platforms.


\end{enumerate}

\section{Concluding Remarks}
This project demonstrates proficiency in the complete data science lifecycle: data
wrangling, exploratory analysis, feature engineering, multi model evaluation,
hyperparameter optimisation, model interpretability, serialisation, and web
deployment. The XGBoost champion model meets the stated accuracy objective
($R^2 = 0.902$), is interpretable via SHAP, and is demonstrably free of gender
and regional bias making it a strong foundation for a production ready insurance
pricing tool, particularly one that could be adapted to serve the growing Ugandan
insurance sector.
